It gained section status in 1989 and remained active throughout the war. It has grown nationwide with local groups having been established in all four regions of the country. 

NEWS

Youth Camp 2016 in Grafton

By Jan posted January 30, 2017

The 2nd nationwide Youth Camp of Amnesty International Sierra Leone was held from 3rd until 6th November 2016 at Grafton Scouts Camp in Grafton, Western Area, Sierra Leone. Among the 42 Participants, we counted 25 boys and 17 girls from throughout the country. The attendees were representing their local school- or community AI – Clubs. Each Province sets 10 young people. The this year's camp was held under the theme


 Empowering youth to take positive action against injustice


The Program of the Youth Camp consisted of seven different sessions about human rights related issues and other activities like the visit of the AI West – Africa Delegation from Dakar, speeches and statements, group works and the election of youth representatives.

While Thursday evening only served as space to register the attendees, to get familiar with the camp rules and to elect commissioners for the position of time – keeper and news – reader, the sessions started on Friday morning after breakfast and the introduction of personalities and statements. 
Alberta Bonney, the Campaign and Growth Director of AISL figured out the role of Youth for AI and the importance of young people for both AI and Sierra Leone. She ended up with the motto: Act – don't just talk. 
Followed by the statement of Hannah Sesay, the former acting youth representative, the Chairman of the Western Area presented the activities of the region, the plans for the future and the recommendations towards the youth and the secretariat. 
Finally Patrick Massaque, the former Chairman of AISL, gave a statement as well.

The first session “Creating a safe space” was directed by Emmanuel Sattie, the Education Coordinator of AISL. The participants were setting rules and behavioral - guidelines within the camp to secure a good and productive atmosphere.

Following Fatma Bockarie, the Maternal Health Coordinator of AISL, gave an introduction into “Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights”. Her power-point presentation both served to awaken the young girls and boys of their rights and responsibilities and to uncover heavy inequity towards Girls' and Women's Rights in traditional Sierra Leone. The clash of traditional gender roles and the idea of gender equality and equal rights became apparent and caused a lively discussion among the youth. Though in the end, the input of Fatma was taken up very good.

The Friday afternoon program was covered by two additional presentations and a group work. 
Emmanuel Sattie was lecturing about “Regional Roadmap for Human Rights Education – Strategic Goals”. In which he illustrated the importance of Human Rights Education pertaining to achieve improvements on the National Human Rights Situation.
The former Chairman of the Board, Patrick Massaque, gave a speech on the increasing “Youth Violence and behavioral change of young people” in Sierra Leone.

The Participants were later asked to sit together in groups related to their provincial affiliation and to prepare a presentation about their activities in 2015/ 2016 and to figure out the challenges they were facing to. The presentations were then held on Saturday and Sunday. Their work (mostly in schools) was hampered due to the Ebola outbreak and insufficient funds. 
As every morning the day started early at 5am with a warm up, followed by breakfast and the first session. 
Mr Brima Sheriff, current director of the NHRC and former Director of AISL, gave a short self presentation before starting his interactive lecture “An Introduction to Social Media and Advocacy – Human Rights and Social Media”. He talked about different types of social media advocacy and how social media can help to build up awareness of and to monitor/ protect Human Rights. 

Introduced by Solomon Sogbandi, the Director of AISL, the regional office for West- and Central Africa from Dakar made a visit. The delegation visited Sierra Leone due to check ups on their ongoing campaign. Beside of meetings with national authorities and press conferences with several NGOs, the team consisting out of four persons took the time to present their current campaign and efforts to lift the ban on pregnant school girls in Sierra Leone to go to school and to give the opportunity to the participants to ask questions. Solomon clarified in his statement the big importance he sees in the youth and youth work. 
Later Mr Gbanie, member of the Human Rights Defenders Network Sierra Leone, gave a session on “Police Accountability”. The session aimed to enhance the knowledge of the youth on public disorder offences and to inform the participants about the dynamics involved in police action and its legal implications, to avoid incidents with the SLP.
Afterwards Emmanuel Sattie gave an introduction into “Human Rights Monitoring, Reporting and Documentation”. The presentation pointed out the importance of Human Rights MDR as well as the guidelines people have to follow when monitoring, reporting and documenting Human Rights abuses. 
Before Jan – Ulrich, intern at the AISL office in Freetown, continued with a session on “Navigating peaceful protests”, discussing about non – violent protest and strategies on how to organize the very, the participants were pleased to vote in different elections for their representative, treasurer, etc… 
The candidates campaigned after getting nominated by their fellow youth. 
The day ended with a talent night.
On Sunday we distributed the certificates and conducted a feedback round.

As a conclusion we can say, the camp was a successful activity. The sessions trained the representatives who can implement the contents into their local projects. It is important to maintain and strengthen the contact between the youth – wing and the secretariat to prevent lacks in communication and the transfer of information. The time the young people spend together was unifying for them and gave new motivation and inspiration to them.
We are looking forward to the next Youth Camp.

WHAT WE DO

Amnesty International Sierra Leone focuses its work on "Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights" especially "Women and Girls' Rights" in Sierra Leone. Further on AI -SL i working intensively in schools and other public educational institutions.
Our Campaigns are set in accordance to Amnesty International's global Campaigns. 

MATERNAL HEALTH

.

 The currently running campaign “Shamed and Blamed” aims to lift the ban on pregnant school girls going to school. Therefore Amnesty International published a report in November 2015 and attracted huge media attention. The ban is not yet lifted and the campaign earns widely support by several local NGOs as well as international organizations and the diplomatic mission of Ireland. 

AMNESTY@SCHOOL

Young people and the school community learn about human rights by putting them into practice every day. Students and Teachers get involved by working together in Youth/ Staff Groups and educate their peers about Human Rights.

Human Rights Friendly School & Youth Groups

A Human Rights Friendly School places human rights at the heart of the learning experience and makes human rights an integral part of everyday school life. From the way decisions are made in schools, to the way people treat each other, to the curriculum and extra-curricular activities on offer, right down to the very surroundings in which students are taught, the school becomes an exemplary model for human rights education.

COMMUNITY CLUB


The Community Club Project is a newly established country-wide "out-of-school" education program which aims to educate ans densibilize both young girls and boys about women and girls' rights as well as sexual and reproductive healthcare. Amnesty tries to clarify young people in this country about these socially hidden topics. The trained transfer their gained knowledge into their peers.
The project works in accordance to the on – going global campaign 'My body, my rights' by the International Secretariat.
The trained will transfer the knowledge they gained through the group - work into their peer – groups.
The clubs get financial support from Amnesty to organise at least one public event per month to gather a broader audience. Events can be radio debates, sport competitions, drama activities, etc…
The section and its cooperating expert groups (e.g. nurses) provide regular trainings (twice a year) to each community club.
